K-12 Specifics: OUs, COPPA & FERPA

In a K-12 tenant, the organizational unit is not just an org chart — it is the policy boundary that lets you treat students and staff as fundamentally different security populations.

Segment students and staff into distinct OUs first

Before you touch a single recovery or 2-Step Verification (2SV) setting, get your population structure right. Most Workspace security controls — account recovery, 2SV enforcement, self-service password reset — are scoped to an OU (or a configuration group). If students and staff live in the same OU, you cannot apply the divergent policies each group requires.

The practical split looks like this:

PopulationRecovery posture2SV / sign-inPassword resets
Students (esp. under 13 / shared)No personal recovery phone or email; self-recovery disabledOften exempt or simplified; no personal recovery channelsAdmin-driven only (see Module 3)
StaffRecovery contacts maintained and verifiedHardware-key (security key) 2SV enforcedSelf-service plus admin reset

This single structural decision is what makes every downstream policy in this module enforceable per-population.

Recovery information is OFF by default for K-12 students

Per Google's December 18, 2024 update: "Adding email and phone recovery information is OFF by default for K-12 users," while it remains ON by default for most users and for K-12 super admins. That default works in your favor — but you should confirm it and lock the behavior down explicitly per OU rather than trusting an inherited default.

The controls live at Security → Account recovery → Recovery information and are OU- or group-scoped. Two important limits: these settings govern Google-managed recovery only, and they do not apply when you use third-party SSO or password sync — in those configurations recovery is handled by your identity provider, not Google.

Why COPPA and FERPA argue against student recovery channels

COPPA (children under 13)

For students under 13, the school provides consent on the parents' behalf. Parents retain the right to access, export, or delete their child's data through the district administrator. Storing a personal recovery phone or email against a young student's account creates exactly the kind of personal data you would then be obligated to surface and delete — so the cleaner posture is to not collect it at all.

FERPA (education records)

Under FERPA, Google operates as a "School Official." Recovery contact information tied to a student account becomes part of the education record, which means you must be able to correct or delete it on request. Every personal recovery channel you attach to a student is one more record element you have to manage, audit, and be able to expunge.

Net guidance for student accounts

For younger and shared student accounts, rely on the admin-driven password reset workflow from Module 3 — not student self-recovery and not personal recovery channels. Keep self-recovery and personal recovery contacts as a staff-only pattern.

Don't attach personal recovery data to young studentsA personal recovery phone or email on an under-13 account is both a COPPA/FERPA liability and an attacker-reachable recovery path. For shared and elementary accounts, the admin is the recovery mechanism — full stop.
Verify in your consoleGoogle publishes the defaults and the underlying principles, but not a turnkey "put students in OU X, disable Y" checklist. Treat OU segmentation as a well-supported best practice, then confirm the current state of Security → Account recovery → Recovery information for each OU — labels and defaults drift, and your tenant may have been provisioned before the Dec 2024 change took effect.
Director takeawayOU segmentation is the lever. Split students from staff first, then layer divergent recovery, 2SV, and reset policies on top. For students — especially under-13 and shared accounts — the admin is the recovery path, and personal recovery channels stay off both for security and for COPPA/FERPA compliance.
