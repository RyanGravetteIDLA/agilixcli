Module 1 · Recovery Readiness

The Director Owns the Recovery Problem

Account recovery is a director-level risk decision, not a help-desk ticket you discover during an outage.

The recurring failure mode

Districts rarely lose access because of a clever attacker. They lose access because of a single point of failure in admin access that nobody planned around. The pattern repeats:

  • The sole super admin leaves — or is terminated, or simply stops cooperating — and no other account can reset their password or demote them.
  • DNS / registrar access was lost. Google's domain-ownership recovery path expects you to prove control of the domain; if you can't touch the DNS zone, that path is blocked.
  • No break-glass account exists, and super-admin self-recovery is off by default for Education tenants. The organization falls back to slow, support-assisted recovery requiring proof of domain ownership.

None of these are exotic. Each is a foreseeable, preventable single point of failure — which is exactly why they belong to the director, not to whoever happens to answer the phone that day.

The reframe: security and recoverability are in tension

Here is the part that catches experienced directors off guard. Every hardening control you enable raises lockout risk.

Hardening controlWhat it raises
Enforce 2-Step VerificationA lost second factor can lock out the account
Restrict to security keys / passkeys onlyA lost or broken key blocks sign-in
Enroll in Advanced ProtectionAccount-recovery options are deliberately narrowed
Disable super-admin self-recoveryThe admin can no longer rescue themselves

This is not an argument against hardening. It is an argument for pairing each control with its recovery counterpart. The director's actual job is to hold both sides of that ledger: do not enable a lockdown without first standing up the recovery mechanism that survives it.

How districts get permanently locked outNo break-glass account + super-admin self-recovery off (the Education default) + a single admin = a district that can be locked out of its own tenant, recoverable only through slow Google support with proof of domain ownership.

What this course covers

Five modules, each closing one of the gaps above:

  1. Recovery readiness — framing, multiple super admins, break-glass accounts, recovery info, and the DNS/registrar dependency.
  2. Super-admin recovery — how to get a powerful account back when it is the one that is lost.
  3. Admin-assisted user recovery — resetting and recovering ordinary user accounts at scale.
  4. Org-wide 2SV / passkey policy — enforcing strong auth without manufacturing new lockouts.
  5. Governance, audit & incident response — monitoring privileged changes and rehearsing the response.
Verify in your consoleSelf-recovery defaults differ by edition and have changed over time. Confirm your tenant's current super-admin and user recovery defaults under Security → Account recovery rather than assuming the Education default still applies.
Director takeawayTreat recovery as a risk you own. Before you tighten any auth control, identify and stand up the recovery counterpart that will survive it — prevention is far cheaper than support-assisted heroics.
