Multiple Super Admins & the Break-Glass Account

One super admin is a single point of failure; the fix is a small, deliberate set of separated admin identities plus a guarded emergency account.

More than one super admin — Google's explicit guidance

Google states it plainly:

“Your organization should have more than one super administrator account, each managed by a separate individual.”

The reasoning is recovery: if one super admin is lost, locked out, or compromised, another human with the same privilege can fix it. A single super admin has no peer to rescue them.

But keep the number minimal

Redundancy and attack surface pull in opposite directions. Google Cloud's super-admin guidance is to retain a minimal number of super admins and discourage everyday use of those accounts. More super admins means more high-value targets and more credentials to monitor.

Verify in your consoleYou will hear “3 to 5 super admins” quoted as a rule. That figure is practitioner consensus, not an official Google number. There is no published target count — size it to your district's staffing and confirm your edition's actual behavior.

Separate admin identity, not your daily account

Super-admin privilege should not ride on the mailbox you use all day. Use a separated pattern:

  • alice@district.org — daily mail, Drive, calendar, the account exposed to phishing and links.
  • alice-admin@district.org — admin tasks only, no daily mail or browsing.

As Google puts it: “Users should only sign in to a super admin account to perform super admin tasks.” Sign in, do the work, sign out. The daily account's larger attack surface never touches super-admin rights.

The break-glass / emergency account

A break-glass account is an industry best practice, not a named Google feature. It is a dedicated, named, rarely-used super-admin account you reserve for the day everything else fails:

  • Secured with hardware security keys plus printed backup codes stored offline (a safe, not a password manager).
  • Excluded from daily SSO, automations, and workflows.
  • Monitored closely — any sign-in is an event worth investigating.
  • It does not count toward your regular admin headcount; it exists for emergencies, not operations.

Under third-party SSO, keep at least one break-glass admin that does not depend on the external IdP. If SAML/SSO breaks, an IdP-dependent admin cannot sign in — the break-glass account using Google-native credentials is your way back in. (This is practitioner best practice, not a quoted Google directive.)

Monitor for privileged changes

New super-admin or privileged-role grants should never be silent. Watch:

Admin console → Reports → Audit → Admin

Alert on new super-admin assignments and role changes. An unexpected grant is either a misconfiguration or an intrusion — both warrant a same-day look.

How districts get permanently locked outA single super admin whose account is also their daily mailbox is two failures away from disaster: phish the daily account, or lose the person, and there is no second privileged human to recover the tenant. Stand up a second separated super admin and a break-glass account before you need them.
Verify in your consoleThe exact “how many super admins” number is not an official Google figure, and recovery behavior varies by edition. Confirm your tenant's super-admin list and recovery defaults directly rather than relying on the 3-to-5 rule of thumb.
Director takeawayProvision a minimal set of separated name-admin@ super admins held by different people, add one offline-secured break-glass account that does not depend on your IdP, and put a standing alert on new privileged-role grants in the admin audit log.
