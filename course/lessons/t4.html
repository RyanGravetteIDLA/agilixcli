Module 2 · Super Admin Account Recovery

The Five Recovery Paths, In Order

A locked-out super admin is recoverable through five documented paths. Knowing the order — and which ones depend on preparation you did months ago — is the difference between a five-minute fix and a multi-day escalation.

Why order matters

The five paths escalate from fast and self-contained to slow and DNS-dependent. The first two resolve in minutes but only if you prepared in advance. The last three are fallbacks that all hinge on proving you control the organization's domain. Work them top-down.

  1. 1. Another super admin resets it (preferred)

    A colleague who holds the Super Admin role resets the locked admin's password through normal user management. Fastest path, no DNS, no Google Support. This is the single most important reason to maintain more than one super admin account.

    When to use: Always your first move. If a second super admin exists and is reachable, you are done in minutes.

  2. 2. Self-recovery via recovery email or phone

    The locked admin goes to https://accounts.google.com/signin/recovery, enters the admin email, clicks Next, then "Try another way." Google sends a verification code to the recovery email or phone configured on the account in advance.

    When to use: When recovery info was set ahead of time and super-admin self-recovery is enabled for your edition. Both conditions are required — and self-recovery is OFF by default for most K-12 editions (covered in L2.3).

  3. 3. Domain-ownership self-recovery (CNAME/TXT DNS)

    When no recovery info exists on the account, Google falls back to proving you control the domain via a DNS record. Covered step-by-step in the next lesson.

    When to use: No second admin, no pre-configured recovery info, but you do control the primary domain's DNS.

  4. 4. Support-assisted recovery

    The formal escalation to Google Support, gated on domain-ownership verification. This is the structured version of path 3 with a support reference number and human follow-up. Walked in detail in the next lesson.

    When to use: The self-service flow stalls or you need Support to complete identity verification after DNS proof.

  5. 5. User-promotion when admins are unreachable

    When every admin is gone or unresponsive, an active user on the domain goes to https://toolbox.googleapps.com/apps/recovery/form, selects "I am a user and cannot contact my administrator," verifies domain ownership via DNS, and requests promotion to Super Admin. Google contacts the existing admins first and only promotes the requesting user if those admins are confirmed inactive or unresponsive.

    When to use: Last resort — no admin can be reached at all, but a legitimate user can prove domain control.

The dividing linePaths 1–2 are fast and depend entirely on prior preparation: a second super admin, or recovery info set in advance. If you did neither, you drop straight to Paths 3–5 — all of which are slow fallbacks that hinge on DNS control of your domain. There is no shortcut once the fast paths are unavailable.
Verify in your consoleGoogle revises both these flows and the URLs behind them. Confirm the current recovery and Admin Toolbox entry points before you need them — not during an outage. The signin recovery flow and Admin Toolbox form labels in particular drift over time.
Director takeawayTwo cheap decisions made today collapse a multi-day lockout into a five-minute reset: maintain at least two super admins, and decide deliberately whether recovery info / self-recovery is enabled for your edition. Everything below Path 2 is a DNS-dependent fallback you do not want to be discovering for the first time mid-incident.
